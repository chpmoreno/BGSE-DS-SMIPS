Distribution of the p-value under the null¶

1 The c.d.f of continous uniform random variable $x$ is given by:

\begin{equation} F(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & x < a \\ \frac{x-a}{b-a} & a \leq x \leq b \\ 1 & x > b \end{cases} \end{equation}

The inverse is then $F^{-1}(y) = a + y (b - a)$ for $0 < y < 1$.

Given $\alpha = 1 - F_H (C_\alpha)$ and $F_H$ is uniform we can use the c.d.f of continous uniform random variable as follows:

\begin{equation} \alpha = 1 - \frac{C_\alpha - a}{b - a} \end{equation}

Then

\begin{equation} C_\alpha = a + (1 - \alpha)(b - a) \end{equation}

and taking $y = 1 - \alpha$ I show that

\begin{equation} C_\alpha = F^{-1}(1 - \alpha) \hspace{2mm} \blacksquare \end{equation}

2 Recall Wasserman (2004) Definition 10.11 and Theorem 10.12:

Definition 10.11. Suppose that for every $\alpha \in (0, 1)$ we have a size $\alpha$ test with rejection region $R_\alpha$. And we have $\Psi$ as a test for single pair hypothesis $\varnothing \neq H \subset \Theta$ (null hypothesis) versus $K = \Theta \setminus H$. Then,

\begin{equation} p_\Psi(x) = \inf_\left\{ \alpha: T(X) \in R_\alpha \right\} \mathbb{P}^*(T(X) \in R_\alpha) \end{equation}

That is, the p-value is the smallest level at which we can reject $H$

Theorem 10.12. Suppose that the size $\alpha$ test is of the form reject $H$ if and only if $T(X) \geq C_\alpha$, then

\begin{equation} p(x) = \mathbb{P}^*(T(X) \in R_\alpha) = \sup_{\theta \in \Theta} \mathbb{P}_\theta (T(X) \geq T(x)) \end{equation}

where $x$ is the observed value of $X$. If $\Theta = \{\theta_0\}$ then

\begin{equation} p(x) = \mathbb{P}_{\theta_0} (T(X) \geq T(x)) \end{equation}

Under Definition 10.11 and Theorem 10.12, let the test statistic $T$ fulfill the monotonicity condition

\begin{equation} \forall \theta_0 \in H: \forall \theta_1 \in K: \forall C_\alpha \in \mathbb{R}: \mathbb{P}_{\theta_0}(T(X) > C_\alpha) \leq \mathbb{P}_{\theta_1} (T(X) > C_\alpha) \hspace{2mm} (1) \end{equation}

Then call $\Psi$ a test such that

\begin{equation} \Psi(x) = \begin{cases} 1, & T(x) > C_\alpha \\ 0, & T(x) \leq C_\alpha \end{cases}(2) \end{equation}

and assume that $\mathbb{P}^*$ doesn't depend on $\alpha$. Then it holds

\begin{equation} p_\Psi(x) = \mathbb{P}^*(T(X) \leq t^*), \hspace{2mm} t^* = T(x) \hspace{2mm} (3) \end{equation}

To proof the above note that the rejection regions $R_\alpha = (C_\alpha, \infty)$ are nested. Then, $\inf\{\alpha: T(x) \in R_\alpha\}$ is attained in $[t^*, \infty)$. The assertion follows from Definition 10.11 and Theorem 10.12.

If $H$ is simple, then $\mathbb{P}_H$ continous and $\Psi$ is defined as in (2), and (3) yields then

$p_\Psi(x) = 1 - F_T(T(x))$, with $F_T$ as in (1). $\blacksquare$

3 Define $X = F(y)$. Let us define the c.d.f. of $X = F(y)$ as $Fx(x)$. Then, for any $x$ in $[0,1]$ we have:

\begin{align} Fx(x) &= P[X < x]\\ &= P[F(y) < x]\\ &= P[F^{-1}(F(y)) < F^{-1}(x)]\\ &= P[y < F^{-1}(x)]\\ &= F(F^{-1}(x)) \\ &= x \end{align}

The distribution that follows the previous c.d.f is the Uniform, hence,

\begin{align*} F(y) = X \sim Unif \end{align*}

4 To prove $p_\Psi (X) \sim_{H} Uniform$ we are going to use 2 and 3. Therefore we can calculate

\begin{align} \mathbb{P}_H (p_\Psi (X) \leq t) & = \mathbb{P}_H (1 - F_T(T(X)) \leq t) \\ & = \mathbb{P}_H (F_T(T(X)) \geq 1 - t) \\ & = \mathbb{P} (U \geq 1 - t) \\ & = 1 - \mathbb{P} (U \leq 1 - t) \\ & = 1 - (1 - t) \\ & = t \end{align}

where $U$ denotes a uniform variate $\blacksquare$.

Multiple testing under independence assumptions¶

1 $y_1, y_2, ..., y_m$ are $m$ independent random variables, it means:

\begin{equation} p(y_1 \cap y_2 \cap ... y_m) = p(y_1) p(y_2) ... p(y_m) \end{equation}

Due to the independence assumption we have then that other events related to them are also independent. For example we can see that:

\begin{equation} p(y_1 > \alpha \cap y_2 > \alpha \cap ... y_m > \alpha] = p(y_1 > \alpha) p(y_2 > \alpha) ... p(y_m > \alpha) \end{equation}

Then using probability propierties and assuming that $y_m \sim Uniform$, then we have that:

\begin{equation} p(y_i > \alpha) = 1 - p(y_i \leq \alpha), \hspace{2mm} i = 1, ..., m \end{equation}

where $p(y_i \leq \alpha) = \alpha$ as we showed in Distribution of the p-value under the null [3].

Therefore under the independence assumption we have:

\begin{align} p(y_1 > \alpha \cap y_2 > \alpha \cap ... y_m > \alpha] =& (1 - \alpha) (1 - \alpha) ... (1 - \alpha) \\ =& (1 - \alpha)^m \end{align}

2 $P(y_i > \alpha) = 1 - \alpha$ is the probability of not reject the null hypothesis. So $\mathbb{P}\left[ \cap_{i = 1}^m \{y_i > \alpha \} \right] = (1 - \alpha)^m$ is the probability of not reject all the null hypothesis. Recall also the exercise before, that is, if there are $m$ independent test and the null hypothesis null are actually true, the probability of at least reject one of the test is

\begin{align} p(\text{At least one error}) &= 1 - p(\text{no error}) \\ &= 1 - p(\text{all null hypotesis are not rejected}) \\ &= 1 - \mathbb{P}\left[ \cap_{i = 1}^m \{y_i > \alpha \} \right] \\ &= 1 - (1 - \alpha)^m \end{align}

3 Let $\alpha$ the overall type I error (reject the null hypothesis when it is true) and let $\alpha_{IND}$ be the individual $\alpha$ of each test then we have the following relationship given the solutions above

\begin{equation} 1 - (1 - \alpha_{IND})^m = \alpha \end{equation}

So we need to find what is exactly $\alpha_{IND}$:

\begin{align} 1 - (1 - \alpha_{IND})^m =& \alpha \\ 1 - \alpha =& (1 - \alpha_{IND})^m \\ (1 - \alpha)^{1/m} =& 1 - \alpha_{IND} \\ \alpha_{IND} =& 1 - (1 - \alpha)^{1/m} \end{align}

A conservative but robust test: Bonferroni¶

1 To begin with, we need to show that $\alpha \leq \mathbb{P}_{C-H} \left[\cup_{i=1}^m \left\{p_i(\mathbf{Y}_i) < \alpha \right\} \right]$. Recall then that $\mathbb{P}\left(p_i(\mathbf{Y}_i) < \alpha\right) = \alpha$ and that $A_j \subseteq \cup_{j=1}^m A_j$ for $j \leq m$. Therefore, we get $\mathbb{P}\left(A_j\right) \leq \mathbb{P} \left(\cup_{j=1}^m A_j\right)$. Then we have that $\mathbb{P}\left(p_i(\mathbf{Y}_i) < \alpha\right) = \alpha \leq \mathbb{P}_{C-H} \left[\cup_{i=1}^m \left\{p_i(\mathbf{Y}_i) < \alpha \right\} \right]$.

Now we have to show that $\mathbb{P}_{C-H} \left[\cup_{i=1}^m \left\{p_i(\mathbf{Y}_i) < \alpha \right\} \right] \leq m \alpha$. For that we are going to use the Boole's inequality $\left( \mathbb{P} \left(\cup_i C_i \right) \leq \sum_i \mathbb{P}(C_i) \right)$. Hence:

\begin{equation} \mathbb{P}_{C-H} \left[\cup_{i=1}^m \left\{p_i(\mathbf{Y}_i) < \alpha \right\} \right] \leq \sum_{i=1}^m \left[ \mathbb{P}_{C-H} \left\{p_i(\mathbf{Y}_i) < \alpha \right\} \right] = m \alpha \end{equation}

2 Using the results above and assuming $p_{(1)},\dots p_{(m)}$ are iid uniform random variables.
